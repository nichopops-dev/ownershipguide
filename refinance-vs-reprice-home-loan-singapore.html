Refinance vs Reprice (Singapore Home Loan, 2026)

Decision comparison · Last updated: February 2026

This is a break-even problem, not a “best rate” problem. Decide with total costs, speed, and how much optionality you need.

Mortgage interest mechanics Start here

The decision in one line

  • If your current bank will offer a good rate with minimal hassle: reprice first.
  • If you need a meaningful step-down in rate, better package terms, or your bank won’t move: refinance.

Reprice vs refinance: what changes

  • Reprice: stay with the same bank; usually faster/cheaper; fewer legal steps.
  • Refinance: switch banks; may require legal work/valuation; can unlock better terms.

How to decide (Singapore)

  1. Compute your “rate gap”: current effective rate vs best market rate.
  2. Estimate total costs: legal fees, valuation fees, subsidies, clawbacks, and time cost.
  3. Break-even: if savings recover costs within ~6–18 months, it’s usually worth serious consideration.

Pitfalls that destroy the savings

  • Ignoring lock-in and early redemption penalties.
  • Missing subsidy clawbacks (if applicable).
  • Optimising rate but accepting a structure that increases fragility (e.g., too aggressive floating without buffer).

TL;DR

  • Reprice if you can get a meaningful rate cut with low friction: minimal fees, quick approval, and your current bank is competitive.
  • Refinance if the market offers a clear step-change: a bigger rate drop, better lock-in terms, or your current bank is not matching — even after negotiations.
  • Don’t decide on headline rate alone. Decide on all-in savings vs all-in costs over your expected holding period.

The only formula you need: break-even time

Think of refinancing/repricing as buying a discount with an upfront cost.

Break-even (months) ≈ Upfront costs ÷ Monthly savings

If you will likely switch/sell/fully prepay before break-even, don’t do it.

What counts as “upfront costs”

  • Legal fees
  • Valuation fee (if required)
  • Bank admin / processing fees
  • Fire insurance / mortgage insurance changes (if applicable)
  • Clawbacks (e.g., subsidy clawback, early redemption penalties)

What counts as “monthly savings”

  • Interest savings from the new package rate
  • Minus any increase in monthly fees (rare, but check)
  • Plus any cash rebates (convert to “monthly equivalent” by spreading over your holding period)

When repricing usually wins

  • Your bank is offering a competitive internal package and you can switch with minimal paperwork.
  • You want to avoid legal/valuation complexity.
  • You expect to refinance again soon and want low friction optionality.

When refinancing usually wins

  • The best market package is meaningfully better (rate + lock-in + flexibility), and your bank won’t match.
  • You want to change package type (e.g., SORA/board-rate structure, lock-in style, prepayment terms).
  • You’re restructuring (e.g., adding/removing borrower, changing tenure) and repricing doesn’t solve it.

Common Singapore gotchas

  • Lock-in vs “effective lock-in”: even with no lock-in, check for clawbacks or penalty clauses.
  • Partial prepayment rules: some packages penalise prepayment above a threshold.
  • Rate mechanics: understand what moves (SORA + spread, fixed step-down, board rate). The risk profile differs.

Decision checklist

  1. How long do you realistically keep this loan? (sell/upgrade/refinance horizon)
  2. What is the all-in cost of switching?
  3. What is the realistic monthly saving (not just the first 3 months teaser)?
  4. What is the break-even month?
  5. Do lock-in / prepayment rules match your likely future actions?
