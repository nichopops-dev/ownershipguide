Is 10-Year COE Renewal Worth It in Singapore? (2026 Financial Breakdown)

Last updated: February 2026

When your COE is expiring, renewing for 10 years feels cheaper than buying a new car. But financially, it is a structural capital decision — not just a cost decision.

1) What 10-Year COE Renewal Actually Means

A 10-year COE renewal requires paying the Prevailing Quota Premium (PQP) at the time of renewal.

If PQP is $90,000, you pay $90,000 upfront. That becomes your new 10-year depreciation base.

Unlike a 5-year renewal, a 10-year renewal:

  • Cannot be extended again
  • Forfeits your PARF rebate permanently
  • Resets depreciation cycle for 10 years

2) The PARF Loss (The Hidden Cost)

If your car is under 10 years old, you may be entitled to a PARF rebate.

Renewing for 10 years eliminates that rebate permanently.

Example:

  • PARF rebate: $12,000
  • PQP: $90,000

Your effective commitment is not $90,000. It is $102,000 of capital exposure.

3) 10-Year vs 5-Year COE Renewal

Factor 5-Year Renewal 10-Year Renewal
Upfront Cost Lower Higher
Extension Possible No No
PARF Loss Yes Yes
Annualised Depreciation Higher per year Lower per year

10-year renewal lowers annualised depreciation but increases long-term lock-in.

4) 10-Year Renewal vs Buying a Used Car

Sometimes switching to a lower depreciation used car creates better capital efficiency.

Compare:

  • Renewal depreciation per year
  • Used car depreciation per year
  • Repair risk difference
  • Liquidity flexibility

If used car depreciation is lower, renewal may not be optimal.

See: Used vs New Car in Singapore

5) 10-Year Renewal vs Buying New

Buying new introduces:

  • Higher capital commitment
  • New PARF lifecycle
  • Higher depreciation curve

If COE cycle is at peak, renewal may dominate new purchase financially.

6) When 10-Year Renewal Makes Financial Sense

  • Car is mechanically stable
  • Low major repair risk
  • COE cycle relatively moderate
  • You want stability over optimisation

7) When It Is Financially Irrational

  • High upcoming repair exposure
  • PARF rebate is large relative to PQP
  • COE cycle extremely high
  • You are over-leveraged

Final Perspective

Renewing COE for 10 years is not “cheap”. It is committing to a new 10-year capital exposure.
