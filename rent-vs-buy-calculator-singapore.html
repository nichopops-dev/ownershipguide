Show breakdown (how this model works)
  1. Buy: you pay upfront (downpayment + buying costs), then mortgage + owner monthly costs. At the end, you sell: net proceeds = sale price − remaining loan − selling costs.
  2. Rent + invest: you invest the upfront cash you didn’t spend buying. Each month, you invest (or withdraw) the difference between buy monthly outflow and rent monthly outflow.
  3. End net worth is compared. This is not a “monthly payment” comparison — it’s a net worth comparison.

How to use this

This page is a planning model. Use it to get a directionally correct answer fast, then confirm final numbers with your bank/lawyer/agent and official calculators where relevant.

Key assumptions (what this model does and doesn’t do)

  • Buy path includes: upfront cash/CPF, mortgage payments, owner costs, and selling costs; it assumes you sell at the chosen horizon.
  • Rent path assumes you invest the upfront amount you didn’t spend on buying and (optionally) invest the monthly difference between owning vs renting.
  • Property price appreciation, rent growth, investment return, and transaction costs drive the result; run at least one conservative scenario.

Worked example

Example: S$2,800 rent vs S$1.3M purchase (5-year horizon)

Try a conservative run: modest appreciation (e.g., 1–2% p.a.), realistic selling costs, and a lower investment return assumption. If buy still wins under conservative inputs, the decision is usually robust.

Common mistakes

  • Mixing up cash vs CPF (they behave differently in approvals and refunds).
  • Using optimistic assumptions (resale value, appreciation, investment return) without testing a conservative scenario.
  • Forgetting one-off costs (fees, penalties, duties) that dominate short horizons.

FAQ

Why can rent win even if “mortgage is similar to rent”?

Because owning has big one-off costs (BSD/ABSD, fees) and opportunity cost on upfront cash. Short horizons punish buying.

What horizon should I use?

Use the minimum time you’re confident you’ll hold. If you might move in 3–5 years, test that first.

Should I include renovation?

Yes if it’s material. Treat it as a cash outflow on the buy path (and a resale uplift only if you’re confident).
