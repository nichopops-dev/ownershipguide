Fixed vs Floating Home Loan (Singapore, 2026)

What this guide helps you decide

This guide helps you choose between a fixed-rate home loan and a floating-rate home loan in Singapore. The decision is not about “guessing rates” — it’s about how much volatility you can tolerate, how long you plan to hold the property, and whether you value certainty enough to pay an insurance premium.

Fixed vs floating in one paragraph

Fixed buys you payment stability for a period (often 2–5 years) but may be priced higher and can have early repayment restrictions. Floating tracks a reference (e.g., SORA) and can be cheaper on average but fluctuates. If payment shocks would stress your household, fixed can be rational even if it costs more in expectation.

Step 1: Know what you’re actually buying

  • Fixed period: how many years, and what happens after (reverts to floating with a spread).
  • Lock-in: penalties for refinancing or partial prepayment.
  • Rate structure: floating = reference + spread; fixed = fixed coupon + later revert.

Step 2: Decide based on household risk

  • Single income / variable income → fixed often reduces stress.
  • High savings buffer → floating volatility is more manageable.
  • Near financial limits → avoid large rate shocks; fixed can be protective.

Step 3: Use a “rate shock” stress test

Instead of predicting rates, test your monthly payment if rates rise by +1%, +2%, +3%. If your budget breaks at +2%, you should value stability more.

Scenario library

  • Scenario A: Planning to sell/upgrade soon — shorter fixed can reduce regret; avoid long lock-ins.
  • Scenario B: Long-term hold — floating may win in expectation if you can tolerate swings.
  • Scenario C: New baby / high commitments — fixed can be worth the premium for peace of mind.

Common mistakes

  • Choosing based on the current rate headline only.
  • Ignoring lock-in and repricing rules.
  • Assuming “floating is always cheaper” without stress testing.

FAQ

Should I fix when rates are high?

It depends. Fixed can protect you from further rises, but if you expect to refinance soon, you may prefer flexibility. Focus on affordability under stress scenarios.

Is fixed “wasted money” if rates fall?

Not necessarily. It was an insurance premium that bought you certainty. The key is whether that certainty mattered to your household.

Rate shock table (how to think)

Even if you don’t know future rates, you can model payment at different rates and see what breaks. A good loan choice is one you can live with under adverse scenarios.

  • If +2% rates makes you uncomfortable → value stability more.
  • If you have ample buffer → floating volatility is manageable.

Fixed vs floating is not a “best rate” question. It’s a cashflow shock tolerance question: what happens to your life if rates move against you for 12 months?

TL;DR

  • Fixed buys stability. Choose it if a +1.0% to +1.5% rate shock would hurt your lifestyle or force bad decisions.
  • Floating can be cheaper on average, but only if you have buffer and a plan when rates spike.
  • If you may sell/upgrade/refinance soon, optimise for flexibility (avoid long lock-ins).

The decision in one line

  • If you need instalment certainty: choose fixed (or partially fixed).
  • If you have buffer and can tolerate swings: floating is viable.

When fixed usually wins

  • You’re near your TDSR edge and cashflow is tight.
  • Your household income is variable (commission/business) and you want stability.
  • You’re already taking other leverage risk (renovation, car loan, dependants).

When floating usually wins

  • You have buffer and can tolerate instalment swings.
  • You expect rates to fall or stay range-bound and want to capture that.
  • You refinance opportunistically and want fewer constraints (check lock-in, clawbacks, prepayment rules).

The real question: what’s your “shock rate”?

Your shock rate is the highest interest rate you can absorb without lifestyle damage or forced selling.

Practical way to choose fixed vs floating

This page is meant to stop the usual mistake: choosing based on today’s headline rate instead of downside tolerance and flexibility.

Decision checklist:

  • If you need predictability (tight budget, single income, low buffer), a fixed period can buy sleep.
  • If you have buffer and flexibility, floating can win over long horizons — but only if you can ride volatility.
  • Don’t ignore lock-in, repricing terms, and fees — they can dominate small rate differences.

Quick decision framework

How to use this page

Use this page to choose between fixed vs floating packages based on your holding period, risk tolerance, and refinancing flexibility.

  1. Decide your horizon (≤2y, 3–5y, 5+y).
  2. Stress-test floating (what if rates rise?).
  3. Factor lock-in/clawbacks and your probability of refinancing.

Scenario library (sanity checks)

  • Short horizon: prioritise flexibility; avoid heavy lock-ins.
  • Long horizon: fixed can buy certainty; check premium paid.
  • Expect cuts: floating may win, but spikes still matter.
  • Write your assumptions (rates, tenure, holding period) in one place.
  • Stress test with a conservative scenario (+1–2% rates, worse resale, higher repairs).
  • Choose the option that still works under stress, not only under optimistic assumptions.
  • Prefer clarity: if you can’t explain the model, don’t commit money to it.

Decision checklist (quick)

Also evaluate flexibility. Some packages restrict partial prepayment or refinancing during lock-in. If you expect to refinance or sell soon, a lock-in penalty can outweigh the benefit of a slightly lower headline rate. Focus on the total package: lock-in, repricing terms, and the revert spread after the fixed period ends.

Trying to time fixed vs floating based on rate forecasts is difficult. Instead, treat fixed as an insurance premium. If you buy insurance, you hope you don’t need it, but you value the protection. If a payment shock would force you to cut essentials, sell investments, or take on expensive debt, then the premium can be worth it.

Deeper dive: why “predicting rates” is the wrong game

  • Use conservative assumptions and avoid decisions that only work in the optimistic case.
  • Prefer clarity and optionality: decisions that keep future options open reduce regret.
  • Revisit big decisions when your life situation changes (income, family needs, rates).

Key takeaways

Finally, prefer decisions that keep options open. Optionality is underrated. A slightly more expensive choice that lets you change course later can be superior to a cheaper choice that traps you.

Another useful technique is to define your “no-regret constraints”: the decision must keep a minimum cash buffer, must not rely on refinancing approval as the only exit, and must not assume best-case market conditions. If a plan violates your constraints, it’s not a plan — it’s a bet.

When you’re unsure, write down three scenarios: conservative, base, and optimistic. For each scenario, list the few variables that matter most (interest rate, resale value, repair costs, rent, fees). You don’t need perfect accuracy — you need a decision that still makes sense when reality isn’t perfect.

More practical guidance

  • Optimism bias: using best-case numbers.
  • Ignoring fees and one-off costs.
  • Forgetting time/effort costs.
  • Changing scope mid-way.

Common decision traps

Small data beats guesswork. The goal is not to predict the future perfectly — it’s to make a decision that keeps you financially safe while meeting your lifestyle needs.

If you’re still uncertain after modelling, take the next step that reduces uncertainty the most. For loans, that usually means getting two competing offers and comparing effective rate, fees, and repayment schedule. For property decisions, it means shortlisting a few realistic units and stress-testing your cashflow under conservative rates. For transport decisions, it means tracking your actual travel spend and time for a month.
