COE Cost in Singapore (2026): Depreciation, Cycle Risk & 5-Year Exposure

Last updated: February 2026

The Certificate of Entitlement (COE) is not a simple “tax”. It is the largest structural cost component of owning a car in Singapore.

In many ownership profiles, COE alone accounts for 50–70% of total depreciation exposure over a typical 5-year holding period.

1) What Is COE (Structurally)?

A COE grants the right to own and use a vehicle in Singapore for 10 years. It is awarded via a bidding system and fluctuates with supply and demand.

For most private car buyers, exposure is primarily Category A or Category B. The key insight is not category differences — it is that COE embeds a large capital commitment into your purchase price, and that capital decays over time.

2) COE Price Cycles (Volatility Is Structural)

COE prices are cyclical. Over the past decade, broad ranges have looked like:

  • Lower cycles: ~$30,000 – $50,000
  • Mid cycles: ~$60,000 – $90,000
  • High spikes: $100,000+

These illustrate volatility — not guarantees. Always reference current bidding results before committing capital.

3) Why COE Drives Depreciation

When you buy a $150,000 car in Singapore, a large portion of that price is COE. Over a 5-year holding period, depreciation is effectively:

COE decay + vehicle value decline.

Example (illustrative):

  • COE component: $100,000
  • 5 years consumed: ~50% of COE lifespan
  • Implied COE usage cost: ~$50,000

That single component can exceed total ride-hailing cost for many households. This is why “instalment affordability” is a weak decision method.

4) The “10-Year” Illusion

Many buyers anchor on “10 years”. In practice, many owners sell within 3–6 years. That means your outcome depends heavily on: your holding period and the COE cycle at entry.

The risk pattern is simple: high COE + short holding period increases downside exposure. If your timeline is uncertain, you should treat this as a risk problem, not a prediction problem.

5) 5-Year COE Exposure Model (Simplified)

If you hold for ~5 years, a simple first-order estimate is that you consume roughly half the COE lifespan. That means the “COE usage cost” over 5 years is often ~50% of the 10-year COE price (before resale nuances).

COE Level 10-Year COE Price Estimated 5-Year Usage Cost
Lower Cycle $50,000 ~$25,000
Mid Cycle $80,000 ~$40,000
High Cycle $110,000 ~$55,000

This is a simplified lens to help you reason clearly. Real outcomes vary with car model, demand, condition, PARF/COE structure, and the cycle at exit.

6) When COE Should Change Your Decision

  • Buying during extreme spikes increases downside exposure. The risk is amplified if you may exit early.
  • Short holding periods amplify COE timing risk. If you cannot hold, consider alternatives that reduce exposure.
  • Borderline cases often tilt away from ownership in high cycles. If you’re near break-even, COE can push the decision.

Final Perspective

COE is not just a line item. It is a macro-level capital allocation decision embedded inside every car purchase.

The higher the COE cycle, the higher your depreciation risk and capital exposure. That is the cost most buyers underestimate — because it is hidden inside the “car price”.

FAQ

How much does COE cost in Singapore in 2026?

COE prices are cyclical and vary by category. In recent cycles, prices have ranged broadly from around $30,000 in lower cycles to above $100,000 during high spikes. Always check current bidding results before making decisions.

How does COE affect car depreciation in Singapore?

COE is embedded inside your car’s purchase price and decays over time. Over a 5-year holding period, roughly half of the COE lifespan is consumed, making COE one of the largest drivers of total depreciation exposure.

Is it better to buy when COE is low?

Buying during lower COE cycles can reduce embedded depreciation risk, but you still need to consider holding period, liquidity, and whether ownership is rational compared to ride-hailing.

Why is COE considered the main cost of owning a car in Singapore?

Because COE is embedded inside depreciation and can account for 50–70% of total depreciation over a typical 5-year ownership period, making it a dominant structural cost driver in Singapore car ownership.
