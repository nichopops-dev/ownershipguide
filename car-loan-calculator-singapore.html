How to interpret this

  1. Separate “can pay” from “should pay”. An instalment can be affordable while total ownership cost is still a bad trade.

Car loan (flat vs effective): why this matters

Many Singapore car loans are advertised with a flat rate. Flat rates understate true cost because interest is effectively front‑loaded. Use this tool to compare loans on a like‑for‑like basis (monthly + total interest).

Quick checks

  • Compare two offers using total paid, not just rate.
  • Include fees — they can flip the decision.
  • Stress test your budget: what if your income dips for 3 months?

Methodology & assumptions

  • Interest model: Uses standard amortisation math (reducing balance). Some offers use different fee structures; verify with your lender.
  • Excludes: dealer fees, insurance add-ons, early repayment fees, balloon/offset features.
  • Use as planning: not financial advice; quotes vary by credit profile and promotions.

Used properly, this page is less about “what is my monthly?” and more about “am I paying too much for the convenience of financing?”.

Simple sanity checks before you sign

The point is not only to know the instalment. The point is to know whether the loan still makes sense once you account for the effective cost of financing. If the effective rate is much higher than the flat-rate headline suggests, the page should prompt a second question: should you shorten tenure, increase downpayment, or reconsider the car altogether?

What the result should change in your behaviour

This calculator is most useful when you plug in numbers from an actual loan quote instead of a generic brochure rate. Many Singapore car-loan quotes are expressed in flat rates, which can make borrowing look cheaper than it feels. This page helps translate that into a more honest monthly and effective-rate perspective so you can compare offers cleanly.

How to use this page for real quotes

Before acting on the result, ask whether the output still makes sense after a conservative stress test. Good calculator use is not about precision to the last dollar; it is about avoiding decisions that only work in the optimistic case. If the answer still holds after you use harsher assumptions, that is usually a sign the decision is robust enough to move forward.

Output checklist

Re-run the calculator whenever one of the major assumptions changes meaningfully: rate, tenure, resale value, rent, energy cost, or your expected holding period. Small updates to these inputs often matter more than trying to make the original run more precise.

When to re-run the model

Use the model to see which assumptions matter most. Then spend your energy validating those assumptions rather than polishing less important inputs.

Even a good calculator cannot fully price convenience, stress, optionality, or the value of keeping your finances simple. What the model leaves out

  • Reading one “base case” as a certainty.
  • Forgetting fees, taxes, or frictional costs that sit outside the neat formula.
  • Using unrealistic tenure or holding-period assumptions.
  • Comparing options that are not truly substitutes.

Mistakes to avoid when reading the output

Run one optimistic case, one conservative case, and one “messy real life” case. The messy case is the most useful: slightly worse rates, slightly lower resale, slightly higher costs, and a shorter holding period than planned. If the decision still looks acceptable, you have a more resilient answer.

Quick scenario ideas

These quick cases are useful because the “cheapest” loan on paper often changes once your own cashflow pattern enters the picture.

  • Short tenure, stronger monthly cashflow: a buyer with stable income may choose a shorter loan and accept higher instalments to reduce total interest paid.
  • Long tenure, lower monthly strain: another buyer may prefer breathing room each month, even if the total financing cost rises, because cashflow resilience matters more than squeezing every dollar of interest.
  • Minimal downpayment, stretched purchase: if the loan only looks manageable with a long tenure and weak buffer, the problem may be the car budget itself rather than the interest rate.
