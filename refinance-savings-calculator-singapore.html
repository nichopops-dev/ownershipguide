What to do with the result

  • Break‑even under ~12–18 months: usually worth investigating seriously (unless you’re likely to sell soon).
  • Break‑even longer than your “likely horizon”: savings may be theoretical.
  • Small monthly savings + large fees: ask for legal subsidy / fee waiver, or consider repricing instead.

How to use this

This page is a planning model. Use it to get a directionally correct answer fast, then confirm final numbers with your bank/lawyer/agent and official calculators where relevant.

Key assumptions (what this model does and doesn’t do)

  • Break-even is driven by (fees + penalties) versus monthly interest savings.
  • Short remaining tenure reduces total interest saved; long tenures make rate differences more valuable.
  • Always model both a ‘best case’ and ‘conservative’ rate path.

Worked example

Example: break-even vs ‘total interest saved’

A deal can have a fast break-even but low total savings if the rate advantage is small. Conversely, a slow break-even can still be worth it if the rate difference is large and you hold long enough.

Common mistakes

  • Mixing up cash vs CPF (they behave differently in approvals and refunds).
  • Using optimistic assumptions (resale value, appreciation, investment return) without testing a conservative scenario.
  • Forgetting one-off costs (fees, penalties, duties) that dominate short horizons.

FAQ

Refinance or reprice?

Reprice is usually lower friction. Refinance can unlock better rates but comes with legal/valuation costs.

Do I include lock-in penalty?

If you are in lock-in, yes — it’s often the dominant cost.

What horizon should I use?

Use how long you realistically expect to keep the loan before changing again (e.g., 2–3 years).

Methodology & assumptions

This calculator is a decision model — it helps you sanity-check trade-offs and stress-test your downside. It is not a quote from a bank/dealer, and it does not include every edge-case fee.

How to use it:

  • Enter your remaining loan balance, remaining tenure, and current rate; then plug in the prospective new rate.
  • Include any fees/penalties you actually face (lock-in, legal subsidy clawback, admin fees).
  • Use the calculator to answer: how many months to break even, and what happens if rates move again.

What the model includes (and does not):

  • Includes: the core cashflow/interest mechanics relevant to refinancing savings estimation.
  • Does not include: promotional packages, negotiated rebates, or your personal credit terms — use your own numbers where possible.
