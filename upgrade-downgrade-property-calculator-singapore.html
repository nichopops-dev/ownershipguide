Using the planner step by step

This page is a planning model. Use it to get a directionally correct answer fast, then confirm final numbers with your bank/lawyer/agent and official calculators where relevant.

Key assumptions (what this model does and doesn’t do)

  • This is the bridge model: it compares your sell-side cash/CPF availability versus buy-side cash/CPF needs.
  • It helps spot whether your constraint is cash, CPF, or borrowing limit (TDSR/MSR).
  • Treat this as a ‘feasibility check’ before you commit to an OTP or timeline.

Worked example

Example: upgrading with high CPF refund

If CPF refund is large, your ‘cash proceeds’ may look fine but your CPF may be constrained for the next purchase depending on timing and balances. Use the pipeline/planner to sequence the steps.

Common mistakes

  • Mixing up cash vs CPF (they behave differently in approvals and refunds).
  • Using optimistic assumptions (resale value, appreciation, investment return) without testing a conservative scenario.
  • Forgetting one-off costs (fees, penalties, duties) that dominate short horizons.

FAQ

What if I sell and buy at the same time?

Timing matters. You may need bridging, temporary cash, or coordinated completion dates.

Do I need to add renovation?

Yes if meaningful — include as cash-only upfront for the buy side.

Where does TDSR/MSR fit?

Calculator notes

Inputs explained

  • Pipeline timing: completion gaps change cash needs materially.
  • Stamp duty: can front-load large outflows.
  • Run one conservative sale-price case and one conservative purchase-price case.
  • Check whether you still have enough buffer after legal, tax, and moving costs.
  • Be honest about whether the move is for lifestyle, cashflow relief, or both.

Checks worth doing before acting

An upgrade is not just a bigger property; it is usually a bigger monthly commitment, potentially lower buffer, and more sensitivity to rates. A downgrade is not just “selling down”; it often unlocks buffer and reduces monthly risk, but can also carry emotional and lifestyle trade-offs. The calculator gives you a financial frame so the emotional side is handled with clearer numbers.

Upgrade versus downgrade: what really changes

This planner is strongest when you treat it as a cashflow and sequencing map, not a promise that the transition will be frictionless. Upgrades and downgrades usually fail not because the idea is wrong, but because one overlooked piece — CPF returned, stamp duty, interim cash needs, or timing — creates pressure at the wrong moment.

How to use the upgrade/downgrade result sensibly

Before acting on the result, ask whether the output still makes sense after a conservative stress test. Good calculator use is not about precision to the last dollar; it is about avoiding decisions that only work in the optimistic case. If the answer still holds after you use harsher assumptions, that is usually a sign the decision is robust enough to move forward.

Output checklist

Re-run the calculator whenever one of the major assumptions changes meaningfully: rate, tenure, resale value, rent, energy cost, or your expected holding period. Small updates to these inputs often matter more than trying to make the original run more precise.

When to re-run the model

Use the model to see which assumptions matter most. Then spend your energy validating those assumptions rather than polishing less important inputs.

Even a good calculator cannot fully price convenience, stress, optionality, or the value of keeping your finances simple. Where assumptions usually go wrong

Run one optimistic case, one conservative case, and one “messy real life” case. The messy case is the most useful: slightly worse rates, slightly lower resale, slightly higher costs, and a shorter holding period than planned. If the decision still looks acceptable, you have a more resilient answer.
