Notes (what this does and doesn’t do)
  • Depreciation is modeled as on‑road price minus your expected resale value at the horizon.
  • Financing uses either an effective APR amortization model or flat‑rate simple interest (common in advertising). Flat‑rate monthly is approximated by adding simple interest across the loan tenure.
  • Fuel uses annual mileage ÷ km/L × fuel price. It ignores rebates/discounts and assumes stable prices.
  • This is a decision calculator, not an accounting statement. Use conservative inputs.

How to use this

This page is a planning model. Use it to get a directionally correct answer fast, then confirm final numbers with your bank/lawyer/agent and official calculators where relevant.

Key assumptions (what this model does and doesn’t do)

  • Depreciation is usually the biggest cost driver; test a conservative resale value.
  • Fuel and parking dominate for high-mileage users and city-centre parkers.
  • Use the breakdown to compare two cars using the same assumptions (apples-to-apples).

Worked example

Example: why ‘cheap monthly instalment’ can be misleading

A car with low instalment but high depreciation can cost more overall than a car with higher instalment but stronger resale. Always compare all-in monthly cost.

Common mistakes

  • Mixing up cash vs CPF (they behave differently in approvals and refunds).
  • Using optimistic assumptions (resale value, appreciation, investment return) without testing a conservative scenario.
  • Forgetting one-off costs (fees, penalties, duties) that dominate short horizons.

FAQ

What horizon should I use?

Use your realistic holding period (3/5/7/10 years).

Should I include COE renewals?

If your horizon crosses COE boundaries, model the COE decision separately.

Is maintenance predictable?

No — use a buffer for older cars and compare scenarios.

How to use this calculator

Use this to convert car ownership into one comparable unit: monthly total cost. This is the number to compare against alternatives (public transport, ride-hailing).

  1. Enter depreciation (or loan + resale assumptions).
  2. Add running costs (insurance, fuel, parking, maintenance).
  3. Use the output as your “true monthly cost”.

Calculator notes

Inputs explained

Once you get a result, compare it against at least one alternative: a cheaper car, a delayed purchase, or a transport mix that uses ride-hailing and public transport instead. A calculator becomes more powerful when it improves the decision set, not just the estimate.

What to do after using the calculator

  • Using instalment as a proxy for ownership cost.
  • Underestimating parking and ERP because they feel small in isolation.
  • Assuming maintenance stays flat over the full holding period.
  • Ignoring opportunity cost of upfront capital.

Common mistakes

Scenario C: older used car. A used car can look great on depreciation but become fragile once you include realistic repairs and downtime risk. This is where the maintenance line matters more than people think.

Scenario B: low-usage lifestyle. If you drive mostly on weekends, the calculator often shows that ownership cost remains heavy relative to use. In that situation, a cheaper car, short-term rental, or ride-hailing mix may dominate.

Scenario A: daily commuter. If commute time saved is meaningful and ride-hailing spend is already high, the calculator may show that ownership is not as irrational as it first looks — but only if you stay within the right vehicle band.

Scenario examples

  • Strong fit: ownership cost still leaves room for savings, buffer, and ordinary life spending.
  • Marginal fit: technically manageable, but only if nothing important goes wrong.
  • Weak fit: car cost crowds out savings, emergency buffer, or flexibility.

Interpretation framework

The output is not meant to say “yes, buy the car” or “no, never buy a car.” It should tell you how fragile or robust your ownership plan is. If the monthly number is only acceptable under optimistic assumptions, the decision is probably weak. If the number still feels manageable under a slightly worse scenario, the plan is stronger.

What this result should tell you

  • Start with honest mileage. Lowballing usage makes fuel, ERP, and parking look artificially small.
  • Use a realistic maintenance number. Older cars rarely behave like brochure assumptions.
  • Test a stress case. Add a repairs buffer, higher insurance renewal, or more parking than expected.
  • Compare at least two vehicles. The result becomes much more informative when you see what actually drives the cost difference.

How to use this calculator well

In Singapore, the biggest car-cost mistakes happen when buyers think only in instalments or only in headline depreciation. Real ownership cost is a stack of recurring and semi-irregular items. This page helps you bring them into one place, so the decision becomes easier to compare against ride-hailing, public transport, or a cheaper vehicle.

This calculator is strongest when you want to compare ownership patterns, not just estimate a single car budget. For example, you can test a newer car versus an older used car, a high-mileage commute versus a low-mileage lifestyle, or a season-parking-heavy setup versus a lighter suburban usage pattern. That makes the result much more useful than a one-number estimate pulled from memory.

What this calculator is best used for

The best calculator result is not the most flattering one. It is the one that still helps when reality is slightly worse than expected. Use conservative assumptions, compare at least two scenarios, and focus on decisions that remain workable without perfect conditions.
